Redistricting and Polarization in Congress

With redistricing underway after the 2020 census there are many concerns about gerrymandering, redrawing congressional districts in favor of one party. In 2010 the Republican party explicitly engaged in this behavior with a strategy known as REDMAP, designed to give them an electoral advantage in the House. Concerns are that both parties will engage in this behavior in 2020 where they have control over redistricting leading to less swing districts.

There are also concerns that this will lead to a more polzarized congress. As a recent New York Times article explored, districts that are easier for one party to win can lead to represenatives with more partisan and extreme views. One measure of partisanship is the DW NOMINATE Scale. This scale goes from -1 to 1, with -1 being the most liberal and 1 being the most conservative (a score of 0 would be a true moderate). Using these scores we can examine whether congress is getting more polarized and if redistricing has had an impact on that.

The Partisan Divide is on the Rise

Over the last few decades House Republicans have become more conservative and House Democrats have become more liberal increasing the divide between the two. However its hard to see any direct impact from gerrymandering.

The lines represent the average score of each member of the Democratic Party and Republican Party. Source: https://voteview.com

Its clear from using nominate scores that the divide in congress is deepening. However its hard to attribute that divide directly to gerrymandering. One way if its had an impact is to look at how each state produced their maps and whether that led to more polarized represenatives from that state. In most states the state legislature draws the map. That legislature can be fully controlled by one party or be under split control. Additionally some states use courts or a commission to draw the maps. If gerrymandering has an impact on polarization we'd expect to see states where the maps were drawn by one party to be more polzarized.

Did Gerrymandering Lead to a More Polarized Congress?

While Republican controlled redistricting lead to slightly more partisan represenatives in congress, Court decided boundaries had basically the same outcome and comissions did little better.

Using courts or commissions to draw districts is often seen as a solution to gerrymandering. The fact that there were among the most polarized districts is a big blow to that theory. Republican drawn districts were also more polarized compared to districts drawn by legislatures controlled by Democrats or that were jointedly controlled.

However, this graph doesn't take into account the partisan leaning of states. We would expect states that are more conservative to have more conservative members of congress while also having legislatures controlled by Republicans. It could be that court drawn districts are in states that are very conservative or liberal as well. Luckily there are various swing states, states that are neither very conservative or liberal, where the districts were drawn using the different methods. We might be able to get a better idea of the impact of these different strategies by looking at those swing states specifically.

In the 2000's polarization in congress had already been on the rise for some time. A darker hue of for a district means that district produced, on average, more extreme represenatives (regardless of party).

That trend continued in the 2010's. We can also see that swing states differed in how ideologically extreme their represenatives were depending on how redistricting was done.

In swing states where the Republican party was in charge of redistricting in 2010, like Florida, North Carolina, Ohio, and Wisconsin, represenatives tended to be more extreme in their views.

Swing states where both parties had some control over the redistricing process, such as Iowa, Maine, New Jersey, and Oregon, tended to produce more moderate represenatives.

Swing states where a federal or state court did the redistricting were a mixed bag. Minnesota and Nevada produced more moderate represenatives, but Colorado and New Mexico produced more extreme represenatives.

Though redistricting through comissions and courts were thought of as a strategy to prevent gerrymandering those strategies seem to have mixed results when it comes to preventing polarization.

It is pretty clear though that Republican drawn districts tend to produce more polarized represenatives. However whether that's a direct result of gerrymandering isn't clear. This election cycle both parties seem set on using redistricting to increase their representation, the impact this has on congress over the next decade will be telling.
