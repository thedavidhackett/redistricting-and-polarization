Redistricting and Polarization in Congress

After every census the each state in the U.S redraws its legislative boundaries to determine the districts that its congress people will represent. These districts can be drawn by a commission, courts, or, most commonly, the state legislature. Since state legislatures are often controled by a single political party a common concern with redistricting is gerrymandering, drawing districts to benefit one party. Historically this has been seen as a tool to increase the representation of one party. In 2010, the Republican party made a conscious effort to gerrymander districts to this end, a strategy they called REDMAP. After the 2020 census there is concern that both parties will engage in this practice in the states where they control the redistricing process. While both parties gerrymandering could be a zero sum game for increasing their power, a recent New York Times article posited that this process is increasing the partisan divide between democrats and republicans. Essentially if districts are easier for Republicans and Democrats to win, ie they aren't swing districts that could go either way, those districts produce represenatives with more extreme and partisan views.

One measure of partisanship is the DW NOMINATE Scale. This scale measures the conservative and liberal voting patterns of members of congress. The score ranges from -1 to 1, with higher scores meaning the represenative is more conservative and lower scores meaning they are more liberal. A 0 would indicate a moderate. Using these scores we can examine whether congress is getting more polarized and if redistricing has had an impact on that.

In the 2000s partisanship had already been on the rise for sometime. A darker hue of purple for a district means that district produced, on average, more extreme represenatives (regardless of party).

That trend continued in the 2010s. But how extreme a states represenatives were in their partisanship could be traced to how redistricting was decided in that state.

In swing states where the Republican party was in charge of redistricting in 2010, like Florida, North Carolina, Ohio, and Wisconsin, represenatives tended to be more extreme in their partisanship.

Swing states where both parties had some control over the redistricing process, such as Iowa, Maine, New Jersey, and Oregon, tended to produce less partisan represenatives.

Swing states where a federal or state court did the redistricting were a mixed bag. Minnesota and Nevada produced more moderate represenatives, but Colorado and New Mexico produced more partisan represenatives.
